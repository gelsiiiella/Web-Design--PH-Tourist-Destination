F O O D S

Indulge in a culinary journey through the vibrant flavors of the Philippines. Imagine yourself savoring the rich aroma of adobo, the tangy sweetness of halo-halo, and the mouthwatering crispiness of lechon. From the bustling streets of Manila to the quaint eateries of Cebu, Filipino cuisine offers a diverse array of dishes influenced by a blend of indigenous, Spanish, Chinese, and American culinary traditions. Join us as we explore the gastronomic wonders of the Philippines!

Adobo

Ingredients: Soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, bay leaves, pepper, chicken or pork

How it is being made: Marinate meat in soy sauce and vinegar, then simmer with garlic, bay leaves, and pepper

Short Background: Considered the national dish of the Philippines, adobo is a savory and tangy stew.

Originated provinces: Batangas, Pampanga

History and Details: Adobo traces its roots to Spanish colonial times, where the cooking method was used to preserve meat.

Sinigang

Ingredients: Tamarind, vegetables (e.g., kangkong, radish, eggplant), fish, pork, or shrimp

How it is being made: Boil tamarind with water, add meat and vegetables, then season to taste

Short Background: Sinigang is a sour soup that's often enjoyed as a comfort food in Filipino households.

Originated provinces: Batangas, Pampanga

istory and Details: HThe sourness of sinigang comes from tamarind or other souring agents like calamansi or green mango.

Lechon

Ingredients: Whole pig, salt, garlic, bay leaves, lemongrass

How it is being made:Spit-roast a whole pig over an open flame until crispy and golden brown

Short Background: Lechon is a festive dish in the Philippines, often served during celebrations and special occasions.

Originated provinces: Cebu, Batangas

History and Details: The tradition of roasting whole pigs dates back to pre-colonial times and continues to be a highlight of Filipino feasts.

Kare-Kare

Ingredients: Oxtail, tripe, peanut sauce, vegetables (e.g., eggplant, string beans, banana blossom)

How it is being made: Stew oxtail and tripe in peanut sauce, then add vegetables and simmer until tender

Short Background: Kare-kare is a rich and flavorful Filipino stew known for its thick peanut sauce.

Originated provinces: Pampanga

History and Details: The dish's name comes from the Tagalog word "kare," meaning curry, and the Malay word "kare-kare," meaning "to curry."

D R I N K S

Embark on a flavorful journey through Filipino beverages, where Salabat's gingery warmth provides comfort, Buko Juice's fresh coconut water quenches thirst, and Sago't Gulaman's sweet tapioca pearls offer a delightful treat. Meanwhile, Lambanog's potent and distinct flavor adds a spirited touch to celebratory gatherings, capturing the essence of Filipino festivity.

Salabat

Ingredients: Ginger, water, brown sugar

How it is being made: Ginger is boiled in water, then sweetened with brown sugar

Short Background: Salabat, also known as ginger tea, is a traditional Filipino beverage enjoyed for its warmth and health benefits.

Origin: Philippines

History and Details: Salabat is often consumed during cold weather or as a remedy for coughs and sore throats.

Buko Juice

Ingredients: Coconut water

Short Background: Buko juice, or coconut water, is a refreshing and hydrating drink made from young coconuts.

Origin: Philippines

History and Details: Buko juice is often served fresh from the coconut, providing a natural source of electrolytes.

Sago't Gulaman

Ingredients: Tapioca pearls (sago), agar jelly (gulaman), brown sugar syrup, water

Short Background: Sago't gulaman is a popular Filipino drink made with tapioca pearls and agar jelly.

Origin: Philippines

History and Details: Sago't gulaman is often served during special occasions and festivities, providing a sweet and refreshing treat.

Lambanog

Ingredients: Coconut sap

Short Background: Lambanog is a traditional Filipino alcoholic beverage distilled from the sap of the coconut flower.

Origin: Philippines

History and Details: Lambanog is often consumed during celebrations and social gatherings, known for its potency and distinct flavor.

D E L I C A C I E S

Indulge in a medley of Filipino delights, from the colorful layers of Halo-Halo to the creamy decadence of Leche Flan and the comforting warmth of Bibingka. Each dish tells a story of tradition and flavor, woven into the fabric of Filipino culture and cherished in every bite. Whether enjoyed on festive occasions or savored as everyday treats, these iconic desserts capture the essence of Filipino sweetness and hospitality.

Halo-Halo

Ingredients: Shaved ice, evaporated milk, various fruits, sweet beans, ube halaya, leche flan, ice cream

How it is made: Layer shaved ice with assorted ingredients and top with evaporated milk and ice cream

Short Background: Halo-Halo is a popular Filipino dessert known for its colorful mix of ingredients and flavors.

Originated provinces: Pampanga

History and Details: The name "halo-halo" means "mix-mix" in Filipino, reflecting its diverse components.

Leche Flan

Ingredients: Egg yolks, condensed milk, sugar

How it is made: Prepare a caramel syrup and pour it into molds. Mix egg yolks, condensed milk, and sugar, then pour into molds and steam until set.

Short Background: Leche Flan is a creamy and decadent Filipino custard dessert often served during special occasions.

Originated provinces: OPhilippines (general)

History and Details: Leche Flan has Spanish origins and is influenced by the traditional Spanish flan dessert.

Bibingka

Ingredients: Glutinous rice flour, coconut milk, sugar, salted eggs, grated cheese

How it is made: Prepare the batter using glutinous rice flour, coconut milk, and sugar. Pour into banana leaf-lined molds, top with salted eggs and cheese, and bake until golden brown.

Short Background: Bibingka is a traditional Filipino rice cake often enjoyed during Christmas season.

Originated provinces: Philippines (general)

History and Details:Bibingka is believed to have originated from pre-colonial times and is associated with Filipino Christmas traditions.

Taho

Ingredients: Tofu, sago pearls, arnibal (sweet syrup made from caramelized sugar)

How it is made: Warm soft tofu is mixed with sweet arnibal syrup and topped with chewy tapioca pearls.

Short Background: Taho is a popular Filipino street food made with fresh silken tofu, sweet syrup, and tapioca pearls.

Originated provinces: Philippines (general)

History and Details: Taho is commonly sold by street vendors in the Philippines, often served as a breakfast or midday snack.
